CAP Guidelines — The Sales Opportunity

CAPSA 2024 didn't just change compliance — it changed the conversation

For 20 years, the question was "should we offer a plan?" The 2024 update made a new question unavoidable: "is our plan actually good enough?"

The old question (pre-2024)

"We offer employees a few fund choices. We've been compliant for years. We're fine."

Result: Low urgency. Hard to open a conversation. Employer defaults to inertia.

The new question (post-2024)

"Are we actually helping employees retire well? Are our fees reasonable? Do we have proper governance documentation?"

Result: Real urgency. Employers need expert guidance. That's your opening.

Every employer with an existing plan is now a potential prospect — not just employers without one. The CAPSA update turned plan governance into a live compliance issue, not a set-and-forget task.

CAP Guidelines — The Advisor's Angle

Five doors the CAPSA update opens for advisors

Each new obligation on plan sponsors is a conversation starter for an advisor who knows the rules.

📋

Governance documentation

Sponsors must now maintain an Investment Policy Statement and document plan oversight decisions. Most SMBs have neither. Offer to build the framework with them.

💰

Fee benchmarking

The update requires sponsors to assess whether fees are reasonable. With CRM3 total-cost reporting arriving in 2026–27, legacy carrier fees will become impossible to ignore. Get in front of it.

📊

Retirement income projections

Sponsors must now consider whether members are on track for income adequacy — not just investment returns. Most current platforms don't surface this. Common Wealth does.

⚡

Auto-features review

CAPSA 2024 explicitly encourages auto-enrollment and auto-escalation. If an employer's plan doesn't have these, the guidelines give you a clear reason to propose an upgrade.

The opener line

"Canada's pension regulator updated its guidelines for the first time in 20 years. It now puts responsibility on plan sponsors to ensure their plan is actually helping employees retire — not just offering investment options. Would you like to see how your current plan stacks up?"

When They Buy

Employers act when something triggers urgency

Most employers don't wake up thinking about group retirement. They act when something disrupts the status quo. Learn to recognize — and create — these moments.

📈

Growth milestones

Hitting 10, 25, or 50 employees often prompts a benefits review. "We're scaling — what do our people need to stay?" is a natural door opener.

🚪

Talent loss / near-miss

A key employee leaves — or almost leaves — citing benefits. Nothing sharpens the mind of a business owner faster than an avoidable departure.

🏆

Competitor offering plans

An employer hears a competitor added a group RRSP. Or they lose a candidate to a company offering retirement benefits. Competition is a powerful motivator.

🗓️

Tax season / year-end

Business owners thinking about their own tax bill often ask their accountant about group plans. Employer matching is deductible. DPSP contributions reduce payroll tax.

📣

Employee requests

Employees asking for retirement benefits — especially in company surveys or 1:1s — put the issue directly on the owner's desk. Validate the ask and come with a solution.

⚠️

Regulatory awareness

An employer reads about CAPSA 2024 or gets flagged by their accountant. They realize their existing plan or lack thereof has a compliance gap. This is your call.

Sales & Set-Up

Why displacements are worth pursuing

An employer with an existing plan isn't a closed door — it's often a better opportunity than a net new sale.

📉

Low engagement is the norm

Most employees with an existing plan take the employer dollars and ignore the rest — no financial planning tools, no engagement with the platform. A displacement is a chance to make the plan a more prominent, valuable part of the benefits package.

💸

Legacy fees haven't kept up

Many SMBs are still on fee structures set when their plan was small. Even as assets have grown, the fees haven't come down. Moving the plan can deliver a meaningful, immediate win for employees — lower fees compounding over decades is real money.

🔧

Displacement fixes underlying problems

Transfers surface issues that have quietly built up: employees in the wrong funds, contribution errors, outdated plan design. Employees typically shift back toward a recommended default fund — which is often exactly where they should be.

🏢

Better service for employers who need it most

Smaller employers without dedicated HR teams are often poorly served by legacy carriers. Modern providers offer meaningfully better support, which matters most for clients with the least internal capacity to manage it themselves.

Handling Objections

The five objections you'll hear most — and how to answer them

"We're fine for now"

Most employers don't think about a plan until someone asks the right question. Bring it up at every meeting — the need is usually there, it just hasn't been surfaced yet.

"We can't afford it"

Employer contributions are completely flexible. Start with a modest match or a fixed dollar amount per employee. You're not committing to a big number — you're building something you can grow over time.

"Sounds like a lot of admin"

Modern platforms have dramatically reduced setup and admin time — what used to take months now takes days. Once the plan is running, ongoing admin is close to zero.

"We're too small"

Technology has lowered the bar significantly. A modern plan is just as easy for a 10-person company to run as a 200-person one. Size is no longer the barrier it once was.

"Why not just give employees a raise?"

A retirement plan gives employees something a raise can't. Contributions are tax-sheltered with no CPP or EI on the way in. Employees get access to low-cost investment funds, built-in financial planning support, and a powerful behavioural advantage — contributions come off the paycheque automatically before they can spend it. That combination is genuinely difficult to match with salary alone.

Putting It All Together

A simple sales flow for group retirement

You don't need a complex process. You need a repeatable sequence that moves a conversation from curiosity to commitment.

1

Open with wellness, not the product

Ask about employees' financial health, stress, and retention challenges before mentioning a plan. Let the employer surface the need — your job is to listen.

2

Bring in the context

Once a pain point emerges, introduce the tailwind: market trends, talent competition, rising employee expectations. Now the plan is a solution, not a pitch.

3

Diagnose before recommending

Ask about their current situation. Do they have a plan? What do employees think? What's their biggest HR challenge right now? Listen more than you talk.

4

Show the platform

A live demo converts faster than a slide deck. You don't need to be a GRS expert — bring in a Common Wealth rep and let them carry the product knowledge. Your job is to open the door.

5

Close on a next step, not a decision

Don't ask for a yes. Ask for a next action: a follow-up call, a plan design conversation, or a demo with the owner and HR together.

This process gets easier fast. After a few conversations, the flow becomes second nature — and Common Wealth is alongside you every step of the way.
